# How Fit Do You Need to Be to Play Golf in Thailand's Heat?

**Less fit than you might think. At the vast majority of Bangkok-area courses, you ride a buggy and a caddie carries your bag. The physical effort of a Thai golf round is genuinely modest — but the heat and humidity are real, and knowing how to manage them matters.**

If you are wondering whether Bangkok's climate rules you out, the honest answer is: probably not. Here is what the experience actually looks like.

---

## 1. What Makes Thai Golf Less Physically Demanding Than Assumed

Two things transform the effort level of a round in Thailand compared to what many visiting golfers expect:

1. **Golf carts (buggies) are standard** — at virtually every golf course in and around Bangkok, a buggy is included or available as standard. You are not walking 6–7 kilometres in 33-degree heat. You drive from shot to shot.
2. **Your caddie carries the bag** — caddies are mandatory at almost all Bangkok courses. Your clubs are carried by someone else for all 18 holes. There is no physical load on your shoulders or back.

The result: a typical round runs 4.5 to 5 hours, most of it spent sitting in a buggy or standing at your ball. For most healthy adults, the exertion is comparable to a slow walk in the park — punctuated by golf swings.

For more on the caddie system, see the [Golf in Thailand Guide](/golf-in-thailand-guide).

---

## 2. The Real Risk — Heat and Dehydration, Not Fitness

Bangkok temperatures sit between 25°C and 35°C year-round. In the wet season (roughly May to October), humidity can exceed 80%. Standing on an exposed fairway in that environment for several hours without preparation is genuinely uncomfortable, and for some people, genuinely risky.

The conditions you need to manage are:

1. **Dehydration** — you sweat heavily without always feeling it in high humidity; fluid loss is faster than it seems
2. **Heat exhaustion** — sustained exposure on open fairways without shade can catch golfers off guard, especially in the first round or two after arrival
3. **Sun exposure** — UV index in Thailand is extreme by European and North American standards; sunburn accumulates fast

None of these is a reason not to play. They are reasons to prepare.

---

## 3. Practical Tips to Manage the Heat

1. **Book an early tee time** — rounds starting between 6am and 9am play in significantly cooler conditions. Temperatures and UV index climb sharply after 10am. See [Best Time of Day to Play Golf in Bangkok](/faq/best-time-of-day-golf-bangkok) for specific guidance.
2. **Drink before you are thirsty** — most courses provide water at every hole or every few holes; use it proactively, not reactively
3. **Wear the right clothing** — lightweight, moisture-wicking fabric makes a real difference; see [What to Wear Playing Golf in Thailand](/guide/what-to-wear-golf-thailand) for specifics
4. **Use shade and the buggy** — stay in the buggy between shots rather than standing in direct sun; it adds up over 18 holes
5. **Eat lightly beforehand** — a heavy meal before playing in heat increases discomfort; eat after
6. **Apply sunscreen generously** — SPF 50+ on face, neck, ears, and forearms, reapplied at the turn

---

## 4. Who Should Take Extra Care

Most recreational golfers manage Bangkok's climate without difficulty once they know what to expect. That said, some groups should be more cautious:

1. **Older golfers (65+)** — heat regulation becomes less efficient with age; extra hydration stops and a later start on cooler-season days help
2. **Anyone with cardiovascular conditions** — consult your doctor before playing in high heat and humidity if you have heart or blood pressure concerns
3. **Golfers arriving from cold climates** — acclimatisation takes a few days; plan a lighter first round if you have just landed from Europe or North America in winter
4. **Anyone who has not played recently** — returning to golf after a long break plus tropical heat is a combination worth easing into

If you are in any doubt, book an early tee time, take the buggy on every hole, and stop if you feel dizzy, nauseous, or unusually fatigued. Thai courses are experienced with international visitors — nobody will object to a slow, comfortable pace.
