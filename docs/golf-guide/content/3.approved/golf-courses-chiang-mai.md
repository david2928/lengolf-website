# Chiang Mai Golf Guide — Courses and Tips

Chiang Mai tends to live in Bangkok's shadow when Thai golfers and visitors plan their itineraries. That's a mistake. Northern Thailand's largest city offers a genuinely different golfing experience: cooler air, courses set against forested mountains and wide valleys, and a pace of life that makes a four-hour round feel far less rushed than anything you'll find in the capital. If you have extra days on a Thailand trip, or you're building a dedicated golf tour, Chiang Mai deserves serious consideration.

---

## What Makes Chiang Mai Golf Different

The single biggest difference between golfing in Chiang Mai and golfing in Bangkok is the climate. Bangkok sits close to sea level in a flat coastal basin; Chiang Mai sits at roughly 300 metres above sea level, ringed by mountains that push above 2,500 metres. That elevation means noticeably cooler mornings, lower humidity during the cool season, and dramatically more dramatic backdrops.

The terrain shapes how courses are designed here. Where Bangkok golf is largely flat with water features and manicured fairways, courses in the Chiang Mai area tend to work with the natural contours of valleys and hillsides. Expect more elevation changes, tree-lined holes that funnel shots, and views that are genuinely hard to replicate anywhere else in Thailand.

Crowd levels are also lighter. Chiang Mai attracts fewer golf tourists than the resort corridors of Hua Hin, Pattaya, or Phuket, which means tee times are generally easier to secure and rounds move at a more comfortable pace — particularly outside peak domestic holiday periods.

---

## Best Time to Visit for Golf

**November to February** is the prime window. The cool season brings daytime temperatures in the low-to-mid 20s Celsius (often cooler still in early morning), low humidity, and almost no rain. This is when Chiang Mai is at its most pleasant overall, so courses will be busier than usual — book tee times a few days ahead.

**March to May** is hot by most standards, with temperatures climbing past 35°C, and the smoke season from agricultural burning can affect air quality and visibility in March and April. Golf is still playable, especially with an early 6–7am tee time, but check the Air Quality Index (AQI) before heading out — a smoke-heavy day makes the experience significantly less enjoyable.

**May to October** brings the monsoon. Afternoon thunderstorms are common and some courses may close fairways or experience drainage issues. Morning rounds are usually fine, and the landscape turns intensely green. Green fees may drop during low season. If rain doesn't bother you and you want the quietest, cheapest conditions, this window works.

## What to Expect on the Course

Green fees at Thai courses generally run **1,500–5,000+ THB** depending on the course, day of week, and season. Chiang Mai courses tend to sit in the mid-range of that bracket — not the premium pricing of a top Bangkok or resort course, but not budget either.

**Caddies are mandatory at most Thai golf courses**, including those around Chiang Mai. The caddie fee is typically **300–500 THB**, paid separately from green fees. A tip of **200–300 THB** at the end of the round is standard practice and expected. Your caddie will read greens, carry your bag, and often provide course knowledge that saves strokes — treat them as a professional.

**Dress code**: collared shirts are required across Thai courses without exception. Shorts are generally accepted. Leave football shirts and vest tops at the hotel.

**Best tee times**: aim for **6–9am year-round** to avoid peak heat and secure the best conditions. In cool season this also gets you the low-light mountain views that photographers rave about.

## Combining Golf with Chiang Mai's Attractions

One of the strongest reasons to choose Chiang Mai over a pure golf resort is the city itself. Few destinations in Southeast Asia pack as much into a relatively compact area.

The **Old City** is a moated square kilometre studded with over 30 temples, including the famous Doi Suthep temple on the mountain above the city. A half-day non-golf day can cover more culture than most week-long resort stays.

The **Sunday Night Market** and **Saturday Night Market** on Wualai Road are worth building your schedule around — street food, local crafts, and an atmosphere that's genuinely Chiang Mai rather than tourist-facing.

The **food scene** is a serious reason to visit in its own right. Northern Thai cuisine — khao soi, sai oua sausage, nam prik noom — is distinct from Bangkok cooking and excellent. Most international golfers spend at least one meal getting acquainted with the local version.

For active rest days, elephant sanctuary visits, cooking classes, and cycling routes through the Ping River valley are all within easy reach of the city.

## Getting to Chiang Mai

**By air**: Chiang Mai International Airport (CNX) receives direct flights from Bangkok's Suvarnabhumi (BKK) and Don Mueang (DMK) airports. Flight time is approximately **1 hour**. Multiple daily departures on Thai Airways, Bangkok Airways, AirAsia, and Nok Air keep prices competitive. Book ahead during the November–February high season.

**By overnight train**: The Northern Line from Bangkok's Hua Lamphong station runs overnight sleeper services to Chiang Mai in approximately **12–13 hours**. A comfortable and scenic option if you're not in a rush; first-class sleeper cabins are good value.

**By road**: The drive from Bangkok is approximately **8 hours** via the main northern highways, depending on traffic out of Bangkok. Viable for a road trip but not the most practical for a golf-focused itinerary.

*Note: transport schedules, airlines, and journey times change — confirm current options at the time of booking.*

## Bangkok vs Chiang Mai for Golf

The honest answer is that they offer different things, and a longer Thailand trip is better served by doing both.

Bangkok gives you [world-class courses near a major city](/guide/best-golf-courses-near-bangkok), high-end clubhouse facilities, and easy access to everything the capital offers after your round. The choice of venues is wider, and some of the prestige courses are genuinely among the best in Asia.

Chiang Mai gives you cooler mornings, mountain scenery, a less commercial atmosphere, and the feeling of discovering somewhere golf tourists haven't fully found yet. The cultural payoff on rest days is also higher here than at a dedicated resort town.
