# Golf Simulators vs Real Courses in Bangkok — Which Should You Do?

Bangkok gives golfers an unusual luxury: genuinely good options on both ends of the spectrum. You can be on a championship course by 7am, or you can be swinging in an air-conditioned simulator bay in the middle of the city at 9pm. Neither is a compromise — they are different experiences that suit different trips, different days, and different goals.

This guide lays out the real differences so you can decide what fits your time in Bangkok.

| **Best for** | Skill practice, convenience, casual sessions | The full Thai golf experience |

## 3. When a Real Course Is the Right Call

If you have a full free day and want the complete Thai golf experience, book a course. Bangkok sits within an hour of 50 or more courses, many of which are well-maintained, scenic, and significantly cheaper than comparable courses in the US, Europe, or Japan.

Real courses make sense when:

1. **You have a dedicated golf day.** A full round runs 4.5–5.5 hours on the course, plus travel. You need to plan your morning around a 5:30–6am departure to reach most courses for a 7am tee time.
2. **You want the caddie and buggy experience.** Caddies are mandatory at virtually all Bangkok-area courses. Many visitors find this one of the highlights of playing golf in Thailand — attentive, knowledgeable, and part of the culture.
3. **Green fees fit your budget.** A typical round ranges from 1,500 THB at a mid-range venue to 5,000 THB or more at a premium club. That still represents strong value for the quality of facilities on offer.
4. **You are playing with a group.** A group round on a real course is a social experience that simulators cannot replicate.

## 4. When a Simulator Is the Right Call

Simulators are not a fallback for when you cannot get to a course. For many situations, they are simply the better choice.

A simulator session makes sense when:

1. **Your schedule is tight.** If you have two hours between meetings or a short evening window, a simulator fits where a real course does not.
2. **You want to practice without logistics.** No early alarm, no taxi to a distant suburb, no waiting on slow groups ahead. You book a bay, walk in, and start hitting.
3. **The heat is a concern.** Playing 18 holes in Bangkok's humidity — especially outside of November to February — is physically demanding. A fully air-conditioned bay removes that variable entirely.
4. **You are travelling without clubs.** Most simulator venues offer club rental, making it easy to play without packing a full set.
5. **You want a late-night or flexible session.** Simulator venues in Bangkok often run into the evening, accommodating schedules that course tee times cannot.

## 5. Using Both on the Same Trip

The most effective approach for a golf-focused trip is to treat simulators and real courses as complementary, not competing.

A common pattern that works well:

1. **Day 1 (arrival or first full day):** Book a simulator session in the evening. Get a feel for the conditions, shake off any travel stiffness, and calibrate your swing without committing a full day.
2. **Day 2 or 3:** Head out to a real course with the warm-up already done. Your first shots on the course will be sharper for it.
3. **Remaining days:** Use simulator sessions for evenings when you want to keep playing without the logistics of another course day.

This approach maximises your time, keeps costs reasonable, and means you arrive at the first tee in better form.

---

## 6. Verdict

There is no universal right answer — the best option depends entirely on your schedule, budget, and what you want from the session.

- **Choose a real course** if you have a full day, want the caddie experience, and are prepared for the early start and travel time.
- **Choose a simulator** if your window is short, you want to avoid the heat, or you need the flexibility of central Bangkok access.
- **Use both** if you are in Bangkok for several days and want to get the most out of your golf.
