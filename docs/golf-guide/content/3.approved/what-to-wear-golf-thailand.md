# What to Wear for Golf in Thailand — Dress Codes Explained

Golf dress codes in Thailand follow the same universal standards you'll find at courses across Asia and the West — collared shirts, appropriate shorts or trousers, no denim. What makes Thailand different is the climate. At 25–35°C year-round with intense humidity from May through October, what you wear becomes less about formality and more about survival. Getting the dress code right means satisfying the pro shop *and* finishing your round without heat exhaustion.

---

## 1. The Rules: What's Required and What's Banned

Thai golf courses — from public pay-and-play layouts to members-only clubs — almost universally enforce the following:

**Required:**
- Collared shirt (polo or golf-specific shirt). Mock-neck and zip-neck designs are generally accepted at most courses, but confirm in advance at private clubs.
- Tailored shorts or full-length trousers. Most courses accept shorts that sit at or above the knee, provided they have a clean, tailored cut.
- Golf shoes or clean athletic footwear (see Footwear section below).

**Banned at most courses:**
- Cargo shorts (pockets and a casual cut disqualify them at almost every course)
- Denim of any kind — jeans or denim shorts
- Collarless shirts, sleeveless shirts, or vest tops
- Gym shorts or athletic-wear shorts not designed for golf

When in doubt, call the course pro shop ahead of your round. Courses sometimes enforce stricter rules for weekend tee times or when tournaments are running.

---

## 2. What to Actually Wear in Tropical Heat

Meeting the dress code is the floor — comfort in Bangkok's heat is what you optimise for above that.

**Moisture-wicking synthetic fabrics** are the standard for a reason. Cotton holds sweat against your skin; modern polyester and nylon blends pull moisture away and dry quickly. Look for fabrics labelled moisture-wicking, Dri-FIT, or similar. This isn't a marketing add-on in Thailand — it's a functional necessity.

**Light colours** reflect radiant heat better than dark ones. White, pale grey, and pastel tones are practical choices, not just aesthetic ones. You'll see a majority of local Thai golfers in light-coloured shirts for exactly this reason.

**Lightweight shorts over full trousers** where dress code permits. Most Bangkok-area courses allow tailored shorts, and unless you're playing a more formal private club, shorts are the sensible choice from April through October.

**Fit matters for airflow.** A slim but not tight cut allows air to circulate. Avoid anything that bunches around the arms or thighs when you swing — aside from the restriction, the trapped fabric adds heat.

If you're playing during Bangkok's hottest months (March–May), tee off as early as possible. The 6–9 am window is noticeably cooler, though "cooler" still means 27–30°C. Afternoon rounds in the dry season can push into the high 30s on exposed fairways.

---

## 3. Sun Protection Gear

Sun protection is where Thai golfers — and experienced visitors — separate themselves from those who suffer through the back nine.

- **Hat or visor:** A wide-brim hat provides the most protection for your face, neck, and ears. Visors are popular for keeping your head cooler while still shading your eyes. Either is acceptable under dress code at virtually all courses.
- **UV arm sleeves:** Extremely common among Thai golfers, particularly women. They cover the forearms and can significantly reduce UV exposure and heat absorption on the arms. Look for UPF 50+ rated sleeves in lightweight fabric.
- **Sunscreen:** Apply SPF 50+ sunscreen 20–30 minutes before teeing off, and reapply at the turn. Exposed areas — back of the neck, ears, forearms — are the ones that burn fastest. Many courses stock sunscreen in the pro shop, but bring your own to avoid running out.
- **Sunglasses:** Polarised lenses reduce glare from cart paths, water hazards, and fairway turf. Check that frames sit securely during your swing.

---

## 4. Footwear

**Soft-spike golf shoes** are the safest choice — accepted everywhere and purpose-built for the turf conditions you'll encounter on Thai courses. Waterproof options are useful during the wet season (May–October) when morning dew and afternoon rain are common.

Some courses — particularly driving ranges and more casual layouts — permit clean athletic trainers (sneakers). However, this varies enough that you should confirm before arriving. Flip-flops, sandals, or heeled shoes are never acceptable.

If you're hiring clubs, many courses can also hire golf shoes, though sizing can be limited for larger Western feet. Bringing your own shoes is recommended for anything longer than a single round.

---

## 5. What to Pack for a Week's Golf Trip

If you're travelling light and fitting golf around other activities, a minimum viable golf wardrobe for a week in Thailand looks like this:

1. **3–4 collared golf shirts** in moisture-wicking fabric, light colours
2. **2 pairs of tailored golf shorts**
3. **1 pair of full trousers** (for any formal or private club rounds)
4. **Golf shoes** (or confirm hire availability at your courses in advance)
5. **Wide-brim hat or visor**
6. **UV arm sleeves** (1–2 pairs, pack small)
7. **SPF 50+ sunscreen** (buy locally if luggage space is tight — it's widely available in Bangkok)

Most hotels with golf concierge services can organise same-day laundry, so 3–4 shirts cover a 5–7 day trip comfortably.
