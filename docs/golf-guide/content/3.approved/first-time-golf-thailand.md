# What to Expect Playing Golf in Thailand for the First Time

Playing golf in Thailand for the first time is a genuinely good experience — the courses are well-maintained, the caddies are attentive, and the combination of early-morning air and tropical scenery makes a compelling case for the sport. But a few things work differently here than at home, and knowing them in advance means your first round runs smoothly rather than being spent figuring out customs under pressure.

This guide covers everything from booking your tee time to handing over the tip at the 18th green.

---

## 1. Booking Your Tee Time

Most Bangkok-area courses allow advance booking by phone, email, or through a booking platform like GolfNow. Some courses are walk-in friendly on weekdays; weekends fill up fast and require advance reservation, particularly at popular layouts.

Book at least a few days ahead for a weekday round, and a week or more for a weekend. For detailed guidance on how to secure a tee time, see [how to book golf tee times in Thailand](/guide/how-to-book-golf-tee-times-thailand).

---

## 2. What to Bring

**Dress code** — collared shirt required at virtually all courses. Tailored shorts or full trousers. No cargo shorts, no denim, no sleeveless tops. Soft-spike or spikeless golf shoes. The dress code is enforced at the pro shop before you go out.

**Quick-reference packing list:**

1. Collared golf shirt (moisture-wicking fabric recommended — it's hot)
2. Tailored shorts or lightweight trousers
3. Golf shoes (soft spikes or spikeless)
4. Hat or visor
5. Sunscreen SPF 50+
6. Cash for caddie tip (see below)
7. Water bottle — though courses also provide water on the course

If you are not bringing your own clubs, rental sets are available at most Bangkok courses. Confirm availability when you book.

---

## 3. Arrival — Pro Shop, Bag Drop, and Caddie Assignment

Arrive at least 30 minutes before your tee time. The sequence at a typical Bangkok course:

1. **Check in at the pro shop** — present your booking, pay green fees and any outstanding charges (caddie fee may be included or billed separately here)
2. **Bag drop** — attendants take your bag to the first tee or the buggy
3. **Caddie assignment** — you will be assigned a caddie. You do not choose; they are allocated by the course. If you have played the course before and have a caddie you liked, you can request them when booking — most courses will try to accommodate this
4. **Buggy** — at most Bangkok-area courses, a golf cart (buggy) is standard. You and your caddie share it, or the caddie walks alongside depending on course policy

Take a few minutes to introduce yourself to your caddie, let them know your handicap or rough skill level, and ask them to help with club selection and green reading. They are there to help.

---

## 4. On the Course — The Caddie Dynamic

The caddie relationship is central to golf in Thailand. At most courses, caddies are mandatory — you cannot play without one. What they do:

- Carry or manage your bag
- Rake bunkers after you play from sand
- Clean your clubs after each shot
- Read putts and advise on line and pace
- Track your ball
- Advise on yardages and wind

You are not obligated to follow every piece of advice — but listening to caddie recommendations on the greens is usually worth it. They know the course far better than you do.

**Pace of play:** Rounds in Thailand typically run 4.5–5.5 hours for 18 holes. This is normal and expected — do not feel pressure to rush. The courses are designed for this pace.

---

## 5. Dress Code on the Course

The same standards from the pro shop apply on the fairway. A few extras worth knowing:

- Mobile phones: acceptable for photos and GPS apps, but keep calls brief and step away from others before taking them
- Silence on the tee: standard golf etiquette applies — no talking during backswing
- Photography: generally fine, but ask before photographing other players or caddies

---

## 6. Tipping at the End

Tipping your caddie at the end of the round is standard practice and expected. The standard range is **400–500 THB per round**, paid in cash directly to your caddie. For a particularly helpful or skilled caddie — especially on a premium course — 600–1,000 THB is appropriate.

Have the cash ready before you get back to the clubhouse. Hand it directly to your caddie, usually with a brief thank-you. This is a meaningful part of their income.

For a full breakdown of tipping norms, see [Thai golf course etiquette](/guide/thai-golf-course-etiquette).

---

## 7. After the Round — Clubhouse and Food

Most Bangkok-area golf courses have a clubhouse restaurant or café. Post-round meals are a genuine tradition — the standard order is iced coffee or a cold beer, a plate of fried rice or pad thai, and a debrief on the round. Most courses offer simple Thai food and some Western options at reasonable prices.

Shower facilities are typically available at the clubhouse. Confirm before you head out if post-round freshening up before an evening plan matters to you.

---

Bangkok's golf infrastructure is well set up for visiting players. The courses are professional, the caddies are experienced, and the whole experience moves at a pace that rewards relaxation rather than rushing. Go early, tip well, and enjoy the round.
