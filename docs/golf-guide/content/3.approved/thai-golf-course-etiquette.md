# Thai Golf Course Etiquette — Caddies, Tipping, Pace of Play

Golf in Thailand is a relaxed, welcoming experience — local courses are friendly to visitors, formal stuffiness is rare, and the atmosphere is generally more social than strict. That said, a handful of conventions differ meaningfully from what golfers expect in the UK, US, or Australia. Understanding them before you arrive means you spend your round enjoying the course instead of navigating awkward moments at the first tee or the 18th green.

---

## 1. Caddies — Mandatory at Most Courses

At the vast majority of Thai golf courses, caddies are not optional. Even courses that technically allow you to carry your own bag will almost always assign a caddie by default, and declining the offer can cause confusion. Treat a caddie as a standard part of your round, not an add-on.

What your caddie does:

1. Carries your bag for the full 18 holes
2. Rakes bunkers after every splash
3. Cleans your clubs after each shot
4. Reads putts and advises on line and pace
5. Gives yardages and advises on club selection
6. Keeps an eye on your ball flight

**How to work with your caddie effectively:**

- Listen to their club recommendations, especially on courses you haven't played before. Thai caddies know their home course precisely, and their yardages and local knowledge are reliable.
- You don't have to follow every piece of advice, but acknowledge it — a quick "thank you" when they hand you a club goes a long way.
- Keep instructions simple and clear. Most caddies speak functional English for golf purposes (yardages, club names, green-reading), but complex conversation can be limited.
- Let them do their job. Raking your own bunker or cleaning your own club after they've moved to do it can feel dismissive.

## 2. Tipping — Amounts, Timing, and How to Handle It

Tipping your caddie is expected at every course. It is not included in your green fee and is always paid directly to the caddie at the end of the round — not through the pro shop.

| Course Type | Recommended Tip |
|---|---|
| Standard public / pay-and-play course | 400–500 THB |
| Mid-range or well-regarded course | 500–600 THB |
| Premium or private club | 600–1,000 THB |

**Practical guidance:**

1. Bring cash in small denominations to the course. Courses rarely help facilitate caddie tips, and ATMs on-site are not universal.
2. Pay at the end of the round, not the start. Handing over a tip before you play is unusual and can create an awkward dynamic.
3. Hand the tip directly to your caddie, not to a cart driver or starter — they are different roles and paid separately.
4. If you've had a cart driver in addition to a caddie, a separate small tip of 50–100 THB is appreciated but not obligatory.
5. If your caddie has gone above and beyond — exceptional green-reading, helping you find several lost balls, strong course management advice — 700–1,000 THB is a generous but appropriate acknowledgement.

Tipping is not a complicated ritual in Thailand. Handing over the cash with a "khob khun" (thank you in Thai) and a smile is perfectly correct.

## 3. Pace of Play — What to Expect

Thai golf rounds run longer than the four-hour benchmark that Western golfers often expect. At Bangkok-area courses, a standard 18-hole round with caddies and buggies typically takes **4.5 to 5.5 hours**. Plan your day accordingly.

Reasons for the longer duration:

1. **Caddie and buggy logistics** — caddies walk alongside or ride a separate caddie cart, which adds coordination time at each hole
2. **Course layouts** — many Bangkok-area courses have longer walks between green and the next tee
3. **Group sizes and weekend traffic** — weekends are busy, and four-balls are common

What this means in practice:

- Book an early tee time (6–7 am) if you want to finish before the midday heat peaks
- Don't schedule a tight afternoon commitment after a morning round
- Keep your pre-shot routine efficient — this is the single biggest factor within your control

Thai courses do not typically enforce pace of play with marshals the way some Western courses do, but being ready to play when it's your turn is basic courtesy that speeds up everyone's day.

## 4. On-Course Behaviour

Thai golf culture is sociable but observes the same fundamental courtesies as anywhere else:

1. **Silence on the tee** — stop conversations when a player is addressing the ball
2. **Mobile phones** — set to silent. Taking a call mid-round is acceptable in a casual context, but step away from the group and keep it brief
3. **Photography** — generally fine on scenic holes, but ask your group's permission before photographing other players' swings. Never photograph during someone's backswing
4. **Divots and ball marks** — replace divots and repair pitch marks on greens. Caddies handle bunkers, but fairway divots are the golfer's responsibility
5. **Cart paths** — follow any 90-degree rule or cart-path-only restrictions, particularly after rain

## 5. Dress Code

Thai courses are more relaxed about dress than courses in Japan or Korea, but standards still apply:

- **Collared shirt required** — polo shirts and golf-specific shirts are the standard. Collarless or sleeveless tops are not permitted
- **No cargo shorts** — the extra pockets and casual cut disqualify them at virtually every course
- **Tailored shorts or trousers** — clean, golf-cut shorts are fine at most courses; full trousers are expected at some private clubs
- **Soft spikes** — metal spikes are banned at most courses. Soft-spike shoes or clean athletic trainers (at casual courses) are acceptable
- **No denim** — jeans or denim shorts are not permitted

If you're unsure about a specific course's rules, call the pro shop before you arrive. For a fuller breakdown of what to pack, see [What to Wear for Golf in Thailand](/guide/what-to-wear-golf-thailand).

## 6. Arrival and Check-In

Arriving on time matters more at Thai courses than at many Western ones, because check-in involves caddie assignment and buggy allocation — both of which take a few minutes.

1. **Arrive 20–30 minutes before your tee time** — this gives you time to check in, pay, collect your caddie assignment, and warm up without rushing
2. **Bag drop** — most courses have a bag drop area near the entrance. Staff will take your clubs and deliver them to the first tee or buggy; you don't need to carry them from the car park
3. **Caddie assignment** — caddies are assigned by the course, not chosen by the golfer. At some courses, regular visitors build relationships with specific caddies and can request them in advance, but this is not the standard expectation for first-time visitors
4. **Green fee and caddie fee** — these are usually paid separately. The caddie fee is typically 400–600 THB paid to the course at check-in, separate from the tip you pay the caddie directly at the end

For guidance on booking your tee time in advance, see [How to Book Golf Tee Times in Thailand](/guide/how-to-book-golf-tee-times-thailand).
